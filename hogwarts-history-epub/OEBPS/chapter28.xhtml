Chapter Twenty-Eight

Staff and Faculty

Staffing Hogwarts has always presented unique challenges. The school requires teachers of exceptional magical ability who are also capable of managing students, administrators who understand both educational and institutional needs, and support staff willing to work in a remote castle far from the comforts of wizarding London. Finding such people, retaining them, and managing their various eccentricities has occupied headmasters throughout the school's history.

Teaching positions have carried varying prestige at different periods. During eras when magical education was highly valued, Hogwarts teaching positions attracted the most distinguished witches and wizards of their generations. During other periods, the positions have been harder to fill, requiring the school to accept candidates who might not have been competitive in better times. The quality of teaching has fluctuated accordingly, with golden ages of pedagogical excellence alternating with periods of mediocrity or worse.

The Defence Against the Dark Arts position has presented particular difficulties. A persistent rumour holds that the position is cursed, that no professor has held it for more than a year since some unspecified event in the mid-twentieth century. Whether this "curse" is real or merely a convenient explanation for the position's high turnover has been debated. What is not debated is the pattern itself: Defence professors have left after a year with remarkable consistency, departing for reasons ranging from death to disgrace to simple exhaustion. The position has become difficult to fill; qualified candidates are understandably wary of a post with such a troubling history.

Other positions have been more stable. The Potions master, the Transfiguration professor, the Charms teacher: these core positions have often been held for decades by single individuals, providing continuity and institutional memory. Long-serving professors become fixtures of the school, their teaching styles and expectations becoming part of what students learn to expect from Hogwarts. The departure of such a professor can be disorienting for students who have never known the school without them.

The caretaker position, responsible for the castle's physical maintenance, has a long history of its own. The role has attracted individuals of various backgrounds: squibs who wish to remain connected to the magical world, former students who could not bear to leave, and occasionally witches or wizards who simply prefer buildings to people. The current caretaker's predecessor held the position for over fifty years, establishing protocols and standards that his successor has largely maintained. The caretaker works closely with the house-elves, coordinating their cleaning and maintenance activities with his own responsibilities for repairs, security, and general building management.

The groundskeeper, responsible for the castle's exterior and grounds, represents another distinctive role. The groundskeeper manages the boundary wards, maintains the Quidditch pitch, monitors the forest edge, and handles various other outdoor tasks that the indoor staff cannot or will not address. The position has traditionally included some responsibility for magical creatures, a connection that has grown stronger over time as the groundskeeper's duties have expanded to include care for the various creatures kept on the grounds.

The matron, responsible for the hospital wing and student health, has always been a crucial but underappreciated position. Hogwarts students injure themselves with remarkable frequency and creativity; managing the resulting medical crises requires both healing skill and strong nerves. The hospital wing itself has evolved from a single room with a few beds to a sophisticated medical facility capable of treating most magical ailments and injuries. The current matron continues a tradition of competence and relative anonymity, working long hours for little recognition while keeping generations of students healthy enough to continue their education.

The librarian guards the school's collection of books, manuscripts, and other textual materials. The library has grown continuously since the founder era, accumulating not only academic works but also rare texts, dangerous books sealed in the Restricted Section, and various peculiar volumes that defy easy categorisation. The librarian must balance accessibility with protection, allowing students to learn while preventing them from accessing materials they are not prepared to handle. Different librarians have struck this balance differently; some have been generous with access, others restrictive to the point of obstructionism.

Staff quarters, scattered throughout the castle, reflect the school's gradual acknowledgment that staff members have lives beyond their professional duties. Early teachers slept in dormitory-like conditions alongside their students. Modern staff enjoy private quarters, varying in size and comfort according to the occupant's status and tenure. These quarters represent the school's investment in retaining good staff, an acknowledgment that capable witches and wizards could find employment elsewhere if Hogwarts did not offer reasonable living conditions.

The challenge of staffing a magical school ultimately comes down to the challenge of finding people who combine rare magical abilities with the temperament for education. Such people exist, but they are not common, and they have options. Hogwarts has succeeded in attracting them through a combination of prestige, mission, and the simple appeal of the castle itself. The school remains, after a millennium, the premier institution of magical education in Britain. That success owes much to the staff who have chosen, generation after generation, to make Hogwarts their home.
