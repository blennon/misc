Chapter Thirty-Six

Headmasters of Infamy

Not all who have held the headmastership have served the school well. Some have been corrupt, using the position for personal enrichment. Some have been cruel, inflicting unnecessary suffering on students and staff. Some have been ideologically committed to causes that proved disastrous. Some have simply been incompetent, failing to perform the basic duties of their office. These failures deserve examination, not to wallow in past errors but to understand how they occurred and how they were eventually addressed.

The name most frequently mentioned in discussions of disastrous headmasters belongs to an individual whose full story remains partially obscured. This headmaster, who served briefly in the mid-twentieth century, was subsequently revealed to have held views and affiliations deeply incompatible with the school's mission. The details of his tenure and departure remain classified under Ministry restrictions that I have been unable to circumvent. What can be said is that his legacy has been thoroughly repudiated by subsequent administrations, and his portrait, if one was ever painted, does not hang in the Headmaster's office.

Elizabeth Burke's headmastership in the sixteenth century ended in scandal when her treatment of the Hogwarts house-elves provoked what records describe as an "uprising." The details are fragmentary, but it appears that Burke attempted to expand the elves' duties beyond the terms of the original arrangement, impose punishments that violated traditional protections, and generally treat the elf population as mere property rather than as partners in the school's domestic management. The elves' response is not fully documented, but Burke resigned under pressure after less than a year in office, and subsequent headmasters were warned against repeating her errors.

Brutus Malfoy's tenure in the eighteenth century lasted only three months before ending in disgrace. Malfoy attempted to use the headmastership to advance his family's political and financial interests, awarding contracts to Malfoy-controlled businesses, manipulating student admissions to favour allied families, and embezzling funds from the school's endowment. His removal required intervention by both the Board of Governors and the Ministry, acting in rare cooperation. The Malfoy family's influence at Hogwarts was curtailed for generations afterward, though it eventually recovered.

Other headmasters have failed in less dramatic but still significant ways. Headmasters who neglected the school's physical plant, allowing buildings to deteriorate. Headmasters who favoured their own houses, creating divisions that took years to heal. Headmasters who could not manage staff, losing talented teachers to frustration and departures. Headmasters who failed to respond adequately to crises, allowing problems to fester that early intervention might have resolved. These failures rarely resulted in dramatic removal; more often, the failing headmaster simply served out their term and was replaced by someone more capable.

The question of how bad leadership was eventually addressed admits no single answer. Sometimes the Board of Governors intervened, exercising their authority to remove a failing headmaster. Sometimes the Ministry stepped in, using emergency powers to impose change. Sometimes staff and students simply endured until the headmaster retired or died. The school's resilience has been remarkable; even the worst headmasters could not destroy what the founders built. But resilience is not the same as invulnerability. Bad leadership caused real harm, to students who suffered under it, to the school's reputation, and to the broader project of magical education.

Safeguards that emerged from these failures include the Board's authority to remove headmasters for cause, the Ministry's power to investigate misconduct, and informal norms that make certain behaviours unacceptable regardless of technical legality. These safeguards are imperfect; determined wrongdoers can sometimes evade them, and legitimate headmasters have sometimes been unfairly targeted. But they represent the school's accumulated wisdom about governance: trust but verify, distribute authority rather than concentrating it, and maintain mechanisms for correction when leadership fails.
