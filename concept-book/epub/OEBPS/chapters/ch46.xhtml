Chapter Forty-Six

Analogy

Structure mapping

Building on: Relation (Chapter 2), Recursion (Chapter 10), Representation (Chapter 22)

"The atom is like a tiny solar system." "Arguments are war." "Life is a journey."

Analogy is seeing that this is like that—two different things sharing a common structure. It's one of the most powerful tools for understanding, learning, and discovering.

What Analogy Does

Analogy transfers knowledge from a familiar domain to an unfamiliar one.

You understand the solar system: massive body at center, smaller bodies orbiting. The atom was once mysterious. By saying "it's like a solar system," you map your solar-system knowledge onto atoms. Nucleus like sun. Electrons like planets. Suddenly you have a mental model where you had none.

This is the power: analogy lets you leverage what you know to understand what you don't.

Surface vs. Deep Similarity

Not all similarities are equal. Some are superficial; others are structural.

Surface similarity: both are round, both are blue, both happened last Tuesday. These are coincidental features that don't necessarily mean the things work the same way.

Deep similarity: both have the same causal structure, the same relational pattern, the same dynamics. These suggest that insights from one domain might genuinely transfer to the other.

Good analogical reasoning focuses on deep similarity. The atom isn't like the solar system because both are "big" or "old"—it's like the solar system because both involve a central mass with orbiting bodies. The structural correspondence is what makes the analogy useful.

Analogy as Discovery

Analogy isn't just for explaining what's already understood—it's for discovering new things.

Scientists use analogy constantly. Darwin saw that artificial selection (breeders choosing which animals reproduce) had effects; by analogy, he proposed natural selection (nature "choosing" which animals reproduce). The analogy led to the theory.

Problem solvers use analogy. Stuck on a problem? Find an analogous problem you've solved. Transfer the solution strategy. Many inventions came from noticing that a solution in one domain could apply to another.

When you're stuck, ask: what is this like? What does it remind me of? What other domain has a similar structure? The analogy might reveal a path.

When Analogies Mislead

Analogies are powerful but fallible. The domains are never identical; the mapping is never complete.

The atom isn't exactly like the solar system. Electrons don't actually orbit in the classical sense. The analogy got physics started but had to be transcended.

"Arguments are war" shapes how we argue—combatively, with winners and losers. But arguments could be collaborative, a shared search for truth. The analogy shapes thought, sometimes in limiting ways.

Every analogy has limits. The prepared mind uses analogies but remembers their boundaries. Where does the mapping break down? What aspects of the target domain aren't captured?

Making Analogies

Analogical thinking can be cultivated.

Collect analogies. When you encounter an idea, ask what it's like. Build a repertoire of analogies that you can draw on.

Study multiple domains. The more domains you understand, the more analogies you can make. Breadth enables transfer.

Focus on structure. When comparing things, look for relational similarity, not just surface features. What's the pattern? What's the dynamic?

Test the mapping. Once you have an analogy, probe it. What corresponds to what? Where does the mapping work? Where does it break down?

Relation Returns

Analogy is fundamentally relational. What transfers isn't things but relationships.

In the solar system analogy: it's not that the nucleus is like the sun (they're physically very different). It's that the relationship between nucleus and electrons is like the relationship between sun and planets. The relations map; the objects are just placeholders.

This is why relational thinking—the focus on connections rather than objects—enables better analogies. When you see the world as relationships, you see potential analogies everywhere.

The Seed

Analogy sees that this is like that. It transfers knowledge across domains by mapping structure. Good analogies focus on deep similarity—relational patterns, not surface features. Analogies are powerful for learning and discovery but always have limits.

The prepared mind thinks analogically. It asks "what is this like?" It draws on a broad base of knowledge to find structural correspondences. It uses analogies while remembering their boundaries. Analogy is the engine of transfer—making knowledge from one place useful in another.
